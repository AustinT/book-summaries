# The Making of a Manager

By Julie Zhuo

Link: <https://www.juliezhuo.com/book/manager.html>

A book about how to manage for new(ish) managers, written from the perspective
of an early Facebook manager. My key takeaways:

- This is the first book I read that stated what the purpose of a manager is:
  "to get better outcomes from a group of people working together." This is a
  better definition than "tells people what to do" or "attends meetings".
- A manager's tasks are quite varied: they need to do whatever it takes to help
  their team get good outcomes. Therefore, you will probably enjoy management
  more if you are motivated by _outcomes_, instead of enjoying specific tasks.
  That being said, one specific task you _need to_ enjoy is talking to people
  (since this almost inevitably becomes a huge part of the job).
- First 3 months of being a manager is a "critical window" when you will be
  given a lot of slack since you are new. Use the time wisely. Ask lots of
  questions and try to learn.
- Hiring and setting cultural examples are some of the biggest impacts that
  managers can have (since they have impact _through their team_ rather than
  directly).
   - When hiring, look for "passionate advocates" (i.e. somebody really wants
     them) instead of "dispassionate approval" (i.e. everybody is positive but
     nobody actually is excited about them).
   - Hire people who are capable of learning and who are capable of more than
     the role requires now (since the role might expand and grow over time).
- The most important basic task you do as a manager is to build a good (and
  trusting) relationship with your direct reports. This will allow them to
  surface problems to you so that you can solve them, and allow you to have
  difficult conversations with them.
- It is critical to _align on expectations_ with your direct reports.
- One on ones should be _about the report_, not about the manager. They are not
  the place for status updates. They are about making the report more
  successful.
- Meetings should have clear purposes and outcomes. Don't be afraid to change a
  meeting that isn't working.
- Not directly about management, but many examples in the book showed that team
  structure/organization/work habits need to change enormously as the team
  grows. Good to know at fast-growing companies!
- Don't tolerate toxicity in your team, period. Even if they perform well you
  should let them go.
- Give feedback quickly and often. You shouldn't just wait for the next
  official performance evaluation.
- Deliver critical feedback _directly_ (i.e. don't use vague language and hint
  at the issue) and dispassionately (i.e. neutral tone, focus on "facts", don't
  say things like "you are thoughtless").
- You need to know yourself well and be honest about your own strengths and
  weaknesses.
- Don't be afraid to give your reports big tough problems to solve. This is a
  sign of _trust_, not a sign that you are too lazy to do the hard work
  yourself.
- Managers should "aim to put themselves out of a job". This won't actually
  mean they won't have a job. There is always more work to do. Instead, it will
  mean they focus on different problems. Therefore, the job of a really good
  manager is basically always changing to focus on new problems, and you stop
  doing the things you get really skilled at.

Overall I thought the book was good and I'd recommend it. Even if you aren't
managing people (e.g. I'm not managing anybody now), it gives a good idea of
what you expect from _your_ manager. There are 2 sides to the manager
relationship, so doing a good job as the report is clearly important too.

The last management book I read was "On Managing People", about 3.5 years ago.
I don't think anything in these 2 books is contradictory, but they definitely
come from two very different authors. Overall I liked this book a lot better.
